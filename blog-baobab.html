Baobab — The Ancient Giant of the Savanna

Plant GuideMarch 2026By Thai Terra Gardens
← Back to Blog

The Baobab tree (Adansonia) is one of the most extraordinary plants on Earth — a living monument capable of surviving for thousands of years, storing vast amounts of water, and transforming any landscape into something truly unforgettable.

Quick Facts

Botanical NameAdansonia digitata (African), Adansonia grandidieri (Madagascar)
FamilyMalvaceae
LifespanUp to 3,000+ years
Height5 – 25 metres (species-dependent)
Trunk DiameterUp to 11 metres
Water StorageUp to 120,000 litres in the trunk
ClimateTropical and subtropical, drought-tolerant

An Icon of Endurance

No tree on the planet commands attention quite like the Baobab. With its swollen, bottle-shaped trunk and sparse canopy of branches that look like roots reaching for the sky, the Baobab has earned names like "the upside-down tree" and "the tree of life." It is native to the African savanna and the island of Madagascar, where several unique species have evolved in isolation.

What makes the Baobab so remarkable is its physiology. The trunk is not wood in the traditional sense — it is made up of fibrous material that acts like a sponge, absorbing and storing enormous quantities of water during the wet season. This stored water sustains the tree through months of drought, and in arid communities, people have traditionally tapped baobab trunks as a source of water during dry spells.

Thousands of Years of History

Some individual Baobab trees are believed to be over 3,000 years old, making them among the oldest living organisms on land. In Africa, ancient baobabs have served as landmarks, meeting places, and even shelters — some large enough to house small rooms inside their hollowed trunks. The famous Sunland Baobab in South Africa once had a pub built within its hollow interior.

Indigenous communities across Africa have relied on Baobabs for millennia. Every part of the tree is useful: the fruit pulp (now internationally recognised as a "superfood") provides nutrients and vitamin C; the leaves are eaten as vegetables; the bark is stripped to produce rope and baskets; and the seeds are pressed for oil.

Why Baobab is in Demand for Modern Landscaping

In the world of luxury landscaping and high-end resort development, Baobab trees have become the ultimate statement plant. No other tree delivers the same combination of ancient grandeur, sculptural silhouette, and sheer visual drama. A single mature Baobab placed in a hotel courtyard, private estate garden, or public plaza instantly becomes a landmark.

Demand is particularly strong in the Middle East, where ultra-luxury real estate developers and hospitality groups seek trees that convey permanence and prestige. Baobabs are also increasingly popular in luxury properties in Australia, southern Europe, and parts of the United States where climates allow.

Growing Conditions and Care

Baobabs are remarkably adaptable once established. They thrive in full sun and require well-draining soil. Their drought tolerance is legendary — once a young tree has established its root system, it requires very little irrigation, making it an ideal choice for water-conscious luxury projects.

  • Sunlight: Full sun; at least 8 hours daily
  • Soil: Sandy or loamy, well-draining; does not tolerate waterlogging
  • Water: Drought-tolerant once established; water young specimens regularly
  • Temperature: Frost-sensitive; best above 10°C
  • Fertiliser: Low-nutrient feeding; over-fertilisation causes rapid soft growth
  • Pruning: Minimal pruning required; the natural shape is the attraction
