Plumeria — The Fragrant Jewel of Southeast Asia

Plant GuideFebruary 2026By Thai Terra Gardens
← Back to Blog

Plumeria — known internationally as Frangipani — is one of the most beloved flowering trees in the tropical world. Its intoxicating fragrance, rich colour range, and elegant form make it the signature plant of resort gardens, temple grounds, and luxury landscapes across Asia and beyond.

Quick Facts

Botanical NamePlumeria rubra, Plumeria obtusa
Common NamePlumeria, Frangipani, Leelawadee (Thai)
FamilyApocynaceae
OriginCentral America and Mexico; widely naturalised in Asia
Height3 – 8 metres
Bloom ColoursWhite, yellow, pink, red, orange, bi-colour
ClimateTropical to subtropical; drought-tolerant

The Most Fragrant Tree in the Tropics

Plumeria is famed above all for its scent — a rich, sweet fragrance that intensifies in the evening and carries on the breeze. Different cultivars produce subtly different fragrances: some are sweet and powdery; others are fruity, spicy, or reminiscent of roses. In Hawaii, plumeria flowers are strung into leis; in Bali, they are offered at temples every morning; in Thailand, the "Leelawadee" is a flower of elegance and spiritual significance.

There are over 300 named cultivars of Plumeria rubra alone, with new hybrids constantly being developed by breeders in Thailand, the USA, and Australia. Thai growers are particularly celebrated for producing rare and exotic cultivars with exceptional colour and scent.

Plumeria in Landscape Design

As a landscape tree, Plumeria is extraordinarily versatile. Its branching, sculptural trunk structure gives it year-round architectural interest even when not in bloom. During the dry season (when many tropical trees are leafless), Plumeria produces its most abundant flowers, creating a dramatic spectacle of colour and scent.

Plumeria is ideal for:

  • Hotel and resort pool gardens
  • Spa and wellness facilities
  • Private estate gardens and courtyards
  • Street planting in tropical climates
  • Temple and spiritual garden settings
  • Rooftop and terrace gardens

Its relatively compact size (3–8m) makes it suitable for spaces where a full-canopy tree would be too large, and it pairs beautifully with palms, bougainvillea, and ground-covering tropical foliage.

Growing Conditions

Plumeria is one of the more forgiving tropical trees to grow and maintain. It thrives in hot, sunny conditions and tolerates drought once established. Its primary weakness is waterlogged soil, which quickly causes root rot.

  • Sunlight: Full sun; minimum 6 hours daily for good flowering
  • Soil: Well-draining sandy or loamy soil; avoid clay or compacted ground
  • Water: Moderate; allow soil to dry between waterings; reduce in cool/dry season
  • Fertiliser: High-phosphorus fertiliser promotes blooming; apply in growing season
  • Dormancy: Drops leaves in dry/cool season; resumes growth in warmth
  • Propagation: Easily propagated from cuttings, which is why Thailand can supply at scale
